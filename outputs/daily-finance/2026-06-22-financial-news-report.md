## Key Financial News
- US equities diverged Monday: the S&P 500 fell 0.37% to 7,472.79 and the Nasdaq Composite dropped 1.32% to 26,166.60 on tech-sector selling, while the Dow Jones added about 148 points as investors rotated into more defensive names ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/21/stock-market-today-live-updates.html), [TheStreet](https://www.thestreet.com/stock-market-today/stock-market-today-dow-jones-sp-500-nasdaq-updates-june-22-2026)).
- Markets remain focused on the fragile US-Iran de-escalation: a memorandum of understanding signed June 17 aimed to end the conflict, but Iran briefly declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on June 20 (citing alleged Israeli violations), a claim the US military denied. Commercial transit through the Strait has been recovering, with crossings climbing to their highest since mid-April ([RFE/RL](https://www.rferl.org/a/iran-war-us-hormuz-oil-blockade-gulf-israel/33640284.html), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis)).
- Oil prices eased toward $74.30/bbl for WTI as easing geopolitical risk and expectations of restored Gulf supply outweighed lingering Strait of Hormuz concerns ([TradingEconomics](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/crude-oil)).
- The ASX 200 closed down 0.15% at 8,815, with tech, mining, and energy stocks leading declines; BHP fell 1.3% and two of the big four banks traded lower, while logistics, industrials, and healthcare provided some offset. WiseTech Global tumbled 12% after police launched a probe into its founder over alleged visa-related misconduct ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/australia/stock-market)).
- The RBA held its cash rate at 4.35% at its June meeting — its first pause this year after three consecutive hikes — with Governor Michele Bullock warning further tightening remains possible if inflation doesn't ease. CBA economists expect rates to stay on hold into 2027 ([SBS News](https://www.sbs.com.au/news/live-blog/rba-june-2026-interest-rates-decision-live/ntpgs9xc6), [RBA](https://www.rba.gov.au/media-releases/2026/mr-26-15.html)).
- In the US, the Fed held its benchmark rate at 3.5%-3.75% on June 17 and removed its earlier projection of a 2026 rate cut, pushing reductions into 2027-2028 as core PCE inflation rose to 3.3% in April, partly attributed to energy-price spillover from the Iran conflict. Markets are now pricing in essentially no chance of a cut this year, with some risk of a hike ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/17/fed-interest-rate-decision-june-2026.html), [REX Shares](https://www.rexshares.com/fomc-june-2026-preview-the-decision-is-settled-the-dot-plot-isnt/)).
- Gold rebounded toward roughly $4,200/oz as easing oil prices and continued geopolitical uncertainty supported safe-haven demand, recouping some of last week's losses.
- Iron ore slipped to 740.50 CNY/tonne (-0.87%) as Chinese port inventories hit record highs amid softer steelmaker demand and rising supply from Guinea's ramping Simandou mine.

## Investment Implications
- The Fed's hawkish recalibration — no 2026 cut and a higher year-end rate projection — raises the bar for risk assets and could keep US bond yields elevated near 4.45%, pressuring rate-sensitive tech and growth names.
- The on-again, off-again Strait of Hormuz rhetoric suggests energy-price volatility is likely to persist even as a formal US-Iran deal framework is in place; investors with commodity or energy exposure should expect continued headline-driven swings.
- The RBA's pause, combined with a softer AUD and falling iron ore prices, points to a more cautious near-term backdrop for ASX-listed miners and rate-sensitive sectors, even as defensive sectors like healthcare and logistics show relative resilience.
