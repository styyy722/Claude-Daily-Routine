## Key Financial News

- **Trump–Xi summit closes with thin results:** After two days in Beijing, Trump and Xi agreed to a "reciprocal tariff reduction framework," established Boards of Trade and Investment, and set a follow-up summit for autumn — but announced no concrete tariff cuts. China agreed to order 200 Boeing jets and to purchase US soybeans and energy, though the Boeing figure fell well short of the 500 planes investors had anticipated; Boeing shares dropped 3.8% on Friday. Xi also warned Trump that any mishandling of Taiwan would put the bilateral relationship "in great jeopardy." ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/15/trump-xi-summit-the-3-big-takeaways-from-historic-meeting-in-beijing.html) | [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/trump-says-china-promised-to-buy-more-boeing-planes-ge-engines))

## Investment Implications

- **Yield risk dominates the near-term macro narrative:** With US 10-year yields at 4.59% and the 30-year at 5% — both levels not seen in over a year — equity valuations (especially growth stocks) face structural headwinds; investors should review duration exposure and consider quality over growth within equities.
- **RBA at 4.35% raises Australian household and credit stress:** Three consecutive hikes tighten mortgage servicing costs significantly; the housing sector (already under scrutiny from the negative-gearing budget proposal) and consumer-discretionary names on the ASX face mounting pressure heading into Q3.
- **Summit "stabilisation" limits China-trade upside but prevents worst-case:** The tariff truce extension and modest frameworks remove the tail risk of renewed trade-war escalation; however, with no concrete tariff reductions, export-oriented sectors (resources, agriculture) will need commodity price support rather than demand-side relief from China.
